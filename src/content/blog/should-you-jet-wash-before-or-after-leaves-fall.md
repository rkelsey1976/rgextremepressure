Autumn is the season most people associate with garden tidying, not driveway maintenance. But the months between September and November do more damage to driveways and patios than any other time of year — and the timing of when you clean makes a significant difference to how much work (and cost) is involved.

## What Leaves Actually Do to Hard Surfaces

Fallen leaves look harmless. They aren't.

Wet leaves pressed against block paving, concrete, or natural stone release tannins — the same compounds that stain a wooden deck brown after a wet summer. On paving, tannin staining soaks into the surface within days, particularly on lighter-coloured block paving and limestone or sandstone flags. Leave them long enough and the staining becomes semi-permanent; it can still be removed with professional cleaning but requires more aggressive treatment and longer dwell times with alkaline detergent.

Beyond staining, decomposing leaves provide exactly the organic material that moss and algae need to establish on a surface. A driveway that was clean in August can have visible green growth by November if leaves have been sitting on it through October.

The worst offenders in Bath and Bristol's tree-lined streets and gardens are:

- **Sycamore** — heavy sticky residue, stains rapidly
- **Lime (linden)** — honeydew secretion that goes black and feeds sooty mould
- **Oak** — high tannin content, dark staining on pale surfaces
- **Horse chestnut** — wet and heavy, holds moisture against the surface for extended periods

## Before or After? The Answer Depends on Your Trees

The straightforward answer is **both** — but if you can only do one, do it **after the main leaf drop has finished**, not before.

Here's the reasoning:

**Cleaning before leaves fall** removes summer algae and grime but leaves the surface exposed to weeks of leaf staining immediately after. You've spent money cleaning and end up with a stained surface by December anyway.

**Cleaning after leaf drop** — typically late October to mid November in the Bath and Bristol area — removes the leaves, the staining they caused, and any algae growth from the autumn damp in a single visit. The surface then goes into winter in good condition.

**If you have significant tree cover**, a clean in September (before the main drop) followed by a sweep and rinse in November after the leaves are down is the most effective approach. The September clean removes summer build-up; the November clean handles the leaf residue.

## Why Autumn Cleaning is Better Than Waiting Until Spring

The argument for "I'll sort it in spring when it's warmer" sounds reasonable but creates a compound problem:

1. Leaf staining sets deeper over winter as the organic matter continues to break down against the surface
2. Moss that establishes in November and December has all winter to root into jointing sand on block paving — by spring, it's entrenched
3. Frost and freeze cycles work on any micro-cracks in the surface, widening them — staining and organic growth accelerate this by holding moisture in the surface
4. A spring clean on a neglected surface takes significantly longer and costs more than an autumn clean on a surface that's been maintained

In practical terms: an autumn clean on a reasonably maintained driveway might take 2–3 hours. The same driveway left until spring after a heavy leaf year could take twice that — particularly if re-sanding is needed on block paving because moss has destabilised the joints.

## Gutters and Driveways: Same Timing Logic

The same principle applies to gutters. Gutters blocked with autumn leaf debris cause overflow, which runs down the face of the building and onto the driveway or patio directly below. Water running from a blocked gutter carries organic matter, roof debris, and grime — all of which contribute to surface staining and algae growth.

Getting gutters cleared in October or November, alongside driveway cleaning, prevents this secondary source of contamination. It's one of the reasons we often combine driveway and gutter jobs at this time of year — they're solving connected problems.

## The Post-Clean Biocide Treatment

Whatever timing you choose, applying a biocide treatment after autumn cleaning makes a significant difference through the wet months. Biocide inhibits algae and moss regrowth; applied after cleaning in late October or November, it typically suppresses regrowth through to the following spring, keeping the surface cleaner for longer and reducing how much work the next clean involves.
