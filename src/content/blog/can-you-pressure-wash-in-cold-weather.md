Winter doesn't stop everything. Gutters still block, driveways still get slippery, and some jobs can't wait until spring. But cold weather does change how pressure washing works — and getting it wrong in freezing conditions can damage the surface you're trying to clean.

Here's a straightforward guide to what's safe in winter and what to hold off until temperatures recover.

## The Core Issue: Freezing Water in Surfaces

The main risk with pressure washing in cold weather isn't the equipment — modern professional pressure washers work fine in low temperatures. The risk is what happens to water after it enters the surface.

Porous surfaces — block paving joints, concrete, natural stone, brick — absorb water during cleaning. In normal temperatures, this water evaporates or drains away within a few hours. In sub-zero temperatures, it freezes. Water expands by approximately 9% when it freezes; trapped inside a joint, crack, or pore, that expansion causes spalling (surface flaking), cracking, and joint failure.

The damage is often invisible immediately and only becomes apparent when temperatures rise — blocks crack, mortar joints crumble, or concrete flakes. It looks like frost damage because it is frost damage, accelerated by the water introduced during cleaning.

## What Temperature Is Safe?

As a working rule: **avoid pressure washing any porous surface when the temperature is at or below 4°C, or when a frost is forecast within 24 hours of cleaning.**

4°C is the threshold because water in a porous surface can still freeze overnight even when the air temperature during cleaning was several degrees above zero. A clear night following a cold day will drop surface temperatures quickly.

In Bath and Bristol, sustained frost periods are relatively rare — the climate is mild by UK standards. But night frosts are common from November through to March, and ground temperature (which affects how quickly surfaces dry) lags behind air temperature. A surface cleaned in January may stay wet for several days in low sun conditions, extending the frost risk window.

## What Can Be Cleaned Safely in Winter

Not all surfaces and jobs carry the same risk:

**Lower risk:**
- **Gutters** — gutter cleaning involves water running through plastic or metal channels, not into porous surfaces. Safe in most winter conditions provided the gutters themselves aren't frozen
- **Smooth, dense surfaces** — glazed tiles, smooth concrete, UPVC fascias and cladding have lower porosity and dry faster; lower risk than block paving or natural stone
- **Render (soft wash)** — soft washing uses low-pressure water with long dwell time biocide; the volume of water introduced into the render is much lower than jet washing, reducing frost risk

**Higher risk — approach with caution or delay:**
- **Block paving** — highly porous joints are particularly vulnerable to freeze-thaw damage after cleaning
- **Natural stone** — sandstone, limestone, and York stone are often more porous than concrete and more susceptible to spalling
- **Roof tiles** — porous clay or concrete tiles absorb water during cleaning; frost following a roof clean can crack tiles or lift pointing
- **Brick** — mortar joints are vulnerable to freeze-thaw cycles

## Equipment in Cold Weather

Professional pressure washing equipment can operate at low temperatures with some precautions:

- Hoses and connections need insulation or need to be drained after use to prevent freezing damage to the equipment itself
- Chemical pre-treatments and biocides work more slowly in cold temperatures — dwell times need to be extended
- Surface cleaners and lances work normally provided the water supply isn't frozen

If you're hiring equipment for DIY use in winter, check that hoses are rated for low temperatures and drain everything thoroughly after use.

## The Slippery Driveway Problem

One reason people look for winter jet washing is a genuinely slippery driveway — black algae or wet moss making a surface hazardous. This is understandable, but the freeze risk doesn't go away just because safety is the motivation.

For an immediately hazardous surface in cold weather, the safest short-term fix is a granular anti-slip treatment or temporary grip tape while you wait for temperatures to improve. A biocide can also be applied in cold weather to kill the algae — it works more slowly than in warm weather but doesn't introduce significant water into the surface. The dead algae can then be brushed or rinsed away when conditions improve.

## When to Book for Winter

If you have a job that needs doing in winter, timing around the weather forecast matters more than the calendar date. A mild spell in December or February — above 7°C day and night for 48 hours before and after — is perfectly workable. We check forecasts before scheduling cold-weather jobs and reschedule if frost is expected.

The best approach for most properties: book a late autumn clean (October–November) before the worst of the cold weather arrives, and use a biocide treatment to suppress regrowth through winter. That way the property goes into the cold months in good condition and you're not trying to schedule cleaning around unpredictable winter weather.
